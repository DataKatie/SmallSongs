Where the Landscape Listens

Dark clouds brood on the horizon:
convolutions of the brain.
The sky knits its brows together,
anxious over the curve of the bay.
Even the highway hugs us close,
the protective arm, clutching tight
across the shoulders of the hills
as the earth bows down in grace.

(and we race on
hard against the wind
in a spray of sand
urged by the chill closing in –
that soft eager breath
whispering in our open ears –
cold settling snug upon our noses
our bare fingers and toes)

We hold each other safe
here, where the landscape listens
and exchange our stories
with the sea. The tides sigh
as though in response, but
they do not speak our language.
We wait patiently for understanding,
for a shower of stars, a dreamless sleep;

here, where the landscape listens
under the round eye
of the watchful moon.

Attitudes of Pearl

She comes to me on these iron-cold days,
hard on the heels of the year’s close.
Today bruised skylines withdraw their mourning
as the scent of decay rises crunching with our footsteps.
Empty-handed, we offer each other our open palms,
but we do not possess a single word between us.

Instead we are language-thieves, snatching
phrases, ringing rhymes, and lost metaphors
as they echo down the years in endless singing rounds.
We hoard each precious syllable as slumbering dragonesses
hidden beneath treacherous mountain ranges
with one eye fixed on cold stolen gold.

Listen. They wait for us behind their words,
Barry, Samuel, and all the Williams,
no longer in the old country where Pearl’s slate
lies broken and bleeding chalk dust
but here, across the ocean in a paper boat
manned with ink blots and wood lice.

They are the dead, but live on
in the mingled measure from the fountain
and the caves, in that dome built in air,
having dared to seize the fire with conviction
while wishing for the cloths of Heaven and
dreaming ceaselessly of Pearl.

I can lay no claim to her, that
windswept spirit waist-deep in the borage.
She is not my accomplice, for
I have stolen her too, now only a
strung necklace of words unravelled
to alphabet in my mouth and bitter burnt ash.

Courtice Road

At Courtice Road

Dust settles in the parking lot
in a gritty layer of memories.
Not like the sedimentary rock,
that great boon to archaeologists,
which houses the past neatly in distinct strata
ready to be excavated, pieced together,
put on display. Even the barest idea
of a breeze would displace these
tiny grains of thought and feeling
that make memory whole.
The tracks written in the dirt
at my feet tell a different story,
as the car merges back onto the highway.
I am left behind. I taste the sand on my tongue,
swallow roughly so it will be part of me
forever after.

Forgotten village of Clarington

What was this once but old farmland
or first deep dark fairy tale forest?
The haunt of the much-maligned wolf,
black bear and soft-footed First Nations tribe
where the sun never fully penetrates
to woodland floor and deep lakes conceal their secrets.
Then forest felled to make way for
foreign farmers, fat livestock and the idea of a country.
We arrived in 1850 to settle here
and give our names to the land.
This land is our land, from the bone of stone
right up to the blue ideal of sky.
We will remain, even to outlast
the old wagon shop on the corner
that now sells cars and fire will not take us
as it did that blackened charred mess
of an eyesore just off the highway.

Directions to Courtice

Gaze into the distance and let your eyes unfocus.
First picture a green Subaru, heavily packed
and alone on the highway.
Imagine the noise of two grandparents
arguing the perfect route
a restless dog barking
the leaking headphones of a Walkman
and the repeated question of, “Are we there yet?
Feel your belly empty because you haven’t yet had breakfast
and note the mounting irritability that accompanies hunger.
This is how to arrive at Courtice.
When each image and sound overlaps
then you will be there immediately
no matter how far away you were before.

Breakfast on the road

Is it time yet? No, never now
until the curve in the road reveals
the branching possibility of diversion
that takes shape as a great old grey barn.
Will we stop? Will we stop?
We will stop. We always stop. We stop.
We stop for greasy bacon and eggs.
We stop for bitter coffee and pulpy orange juice.
We stop to let the dog run in crazed, glorious circles.
We stop for the comfort of tradition.
We stop to say that we have reached the first stop.
We stop to marvel that the place has not changed.
We stop because it is a place on the map we recognize.
We stop to remember that we used to stop here once.
We stop to mourn what is no longer here.

A short history of Courtice

I do not know its past except
where it overlaps with my own.
We only knew each other casually,
this place and I, meeting twice a year
for breakfast on the return voyage north.
These meals were sleepy affairs with little
in the way of conversation.
I never asked if it had been a farm,
once, or what its aspirations were
if in the future it should be a restaurant no longer.
For its part Courtice never asked me
where I was going or if
I liked the eggs.

The time traveller’s dilemma

Praise the dandelion clock, that
bends the facts of time to its own imaginings:
each puff strikes the hour and propels
a new once upon a time for every traveller.
I can journey forward or back in time
on only the power of thought, except
the measure of control is an illusion
and it is impossible to say to when I am going.
What will I find when I arrive?
It could be a small girl and her dog
or clusters of strangers clamouring to be fed.
I could suffocate in the dense smoke
of fire uncontrolled or loose myself in the mystery
of glacial prehistory, with only my bones
surviving to tell my story.

Portrait of Courtice

I could paint Courtice
as if it were a landscape
by one of the Group of Seven –
a landmark in memory softened
in contour, boundary, and colour
as it filters through the lens of feeling
buffeted by the vagaries of time
until it becomes a scraggly pine tree
desperately clinging to a barren rock.
Instead I will attempt a portrait
to reinvent the revenant.
If I resurrect the long neck of the road
as it curves to meet the tilt of the sharp-angled
barn-door chin in perfect detail
I will bring it to life once more
to exist under the scrutiny of my jealous guard.

Missing Courtice

You are not the one I miss
with the spring-thaw flood
of heartache sealed tight
behind a dam of forget.
I can live without you, Courtice.
If I wanted, I could think
never again of the nothing you are
to me, except that when I return
to you, there she is waiting for me,
the top of her grey head
barely visible above the steering wheel
and the dog running happy laps
around and around the open car.
But when I go to leave you behind
forever, I will be left with only
ringing barks echoing in my ears.

Directions to Breakfast

This is how to arrive at Courtice.
First picture greasy bacon and eggs,
bitter coffee and pulpy orange juice,
with a side of mounting irritability.
Then ask the question, “Are we there yet?”
Will we stop? Are we there yet?
We’re there when the restless dog is barking.
We’re there when the green Subaru is on the highway.
We’re there when both grandparents argue the perfect route.
We’re there when Courtice has not changed.
We’re there when it becomes a place we no longer recognize.
We’re there to take comfort in tradition.
When will we stop? Will we stop?

The time traveller arrives at a forgotten village

What was this once but a
praised dandelion clock, lying spent
in old farmland or deep dark fairy tale forest?
Bending the facts of time to its own imagining,
the hour strikes anew in the haunt of
much-maligned wolf and soft-footed First Nations tribe.
I can journey here where the sun never fully penetrates
to woodland floor on only the power of thought
when we arrived in 1850 to settle here
a cluster of strangers clamouring to be fed
and give our names to the land.
This land our land from glacial prehistory
until the suffocating dense smoke of fire uncontrolled
outlasts the old wagon shop on the corner
and that small girl and her dog.

Courtice Road Forgotten

Sedimentary rock praises dandelion clock
layered strata reveal nothing
no real boon to archaeologists.
Image and sound overlaps past and present,
arriving in 1850 to outlast glacial prehistory.
A tiny breeze displaces these.
Foreign farmers fell first forest
and taste sand on their tongues.
Headphones leak restless dog barking.
Alone on the highway
the curve of the road reveals
fire uncontrolled.
Deep lakes conceal surviving bones.

At Courtice Road

I am left behind. I leave
to leave you behind forever
muttering, “Are we there yet?”
over the noise of the perfect route.
Alone on the highway, I gaze into the distance.
The road reveals the branching possibility
of journeying forward or back in time
without aspirations or the desire for eggs.
We only knew each other casually.
I will filter through the lens of feeling
to arrive at a place on the map I recognize
dense smoke shrouding tradition in comfort.
Breakfast in the forgotten village
Car merges to scraggly pine tree
though the highway desperately clings
to tradition and navigates the perfect route
through a wind of forget.
Even the map wouldn’t recognize this place –
farmland grows rock for crop
and trees march inside the barn, ousting cattle.
The restaurant endures bravely,
serving up breakfast peppered
with the dust of memory
a picnic in the wildwood wolf haunt.
The only constant is the dog barking.
The eggs burn in a fire
that may not have happened yet.

Dear Courtice

No, you were right to say
that summer road trips across
South-Eastern Ontario would not
be the same without you.
It was not your brazen confidence
in my attachment to you
that precipitated my long silence.
There is nothing left to say.
I do not go your way anymore;
you are changed beyond recognition.
One day the light lying
across an empty highway at dawn
will bring passing thought of you.
Do not write me again.
